# Product Marketing

Positioning and messaging is a running accountability, not a launch-week scramble. The moment a product-spec outcome or a Job Spec changes, the story the market hears should change with it. This page is who owns that, when it happens, and how the copy reaches sales and the website.

## Who owns it

Ownership splits by team size, and naming the split early avoids the gap where nobody does it:

- **In a larger team, the PMM owns it.** A product marketing manager owns the copy kit, keeps it current as the specs move, and feeds sales enablement and the website.
- **In a smaller team, the PM owns it.** Where there is no PMM, this is the PM's job, carried alongside the spec work. It is not optional and it is not "marketing's problem later". The person who owns the specs owns the projection of them.

Either way, one named person owns the copy kit, or it is nobody's and it goes stale.

> **Two layers, one split.** The [Positioning and Messaging guide](../guides/positioning-and-messaging.md) is the **method**: how to derive pillars, the wedge, proof, and voice from the specs. The [Copy Kit template](../templates/copy-kit.md) is the **artefact**: the fill-in COPY-KIT.md a product keeps next to its specs. This page is the **accountability**: who owns the kit, when it is refreshed, and how it feeds sales and the site. When you need to write the copy, go to the guide. When you need to know what you are on the hook for, stay here.

## What the discipline owns

Three things, in order of how often they bite.

- **Keeping the copy kit current as the specs evolve.** When a product-spec
  outcome changes, its pillar changes. When a Job Spec's *Today's alternatives*
  or *Prove it* changes, the wedge and the proof points change. The messaging
  follows the specs; it does not drift on its own.
- **Running the refresh on a cadence**, not only at launch. A quarterly pass
  over the kit against the current specs catches the pillars that no longer match
  a live outcome and the proof points whose Prove-it scenario was retired.
- **Feeding the downstream surfaces.** Sales enablement, decks, the website, the
  README, and launch comms all lift from the kit. The discipline owns that the
  kit is the single source and the surfaces read from it rather than each
  inventing their own version.

## What good looks like

A team that markets from the specs and a team that invents taglines in a vacuum are easy to tell apart.

**A team that markets from the specs:**

- Every pillar names the outcome it traces to, and every proof point names its Prove-it scenario.
- When an outcome or a Job Spec changes, the copy kit is updated in the same breath, not a quarter later.
- Sales, the website, and the README all say the same thing, because they all lift from one kit.
- The wedge names the real alternative a buyer uses today, so a demo confirms the story instead of exposing it.
- A claim that cannot be sourced to a spec gets cut, not shipped.

**A team that invents in a vacuum:**

- Taglines are written in a workshop, disconnected from any outcome.
- The deck, the site, and the README each say something different, and nobody knows which is current.
- Proof points are numbers someone made up because the slide needed one.
- The messaging still describes last year's product because nobody refreshed it when the spec moved.
- The wedge is a strawman, so the story falls apart the moment a buyer names their real alternative.

The tell is the orphan claim. **If a line of copy cannot be traced back to an outcome, an alternative, or a Prove-it, the team is marketing to a story, not to the product.**

## How it fits the playbook loop

Messaging is a step in the loop, downstream of the specs and upstream of the sell:

- **[Discovery](./discovery.md)** produces the personas and the job stories. The
  voice of customer comes straight from there: the words a buyer recognises are
  already in the struggling moment.
- **The specs** ([product spec](../templates/product-spec.md) outcomes and
  [Job Specs](../templates/job-spec.md)) are the source the copy kit projects.
  No spec, no messaging: an outcome you cannot name is a pillar you cannot write.
- **Messaging** projects the specs into the copy kit, which every customer-facing
  surface then lifts from.
- **Sell**: sales enablement, decks, the website, and launch comms are the
  surfaces the kit feeds.

Timing rides on the release rhythm. The messaging is ready **before GA**, not after: [release phases](./release-phases.md) push a product through private and public preview, and the copy kit should be current by the time GA opens the doors. The launch and post-launch rhythm then reads the same specs, so a post-launch change to an outcome loops straight back into a copy-kit refresh.

## The message is a scored call

Positioning is a prediction: *this framing will move the funnel*. Treat it like one. Every launch narrative ships with a predicted funnel movement and a date ("stage-1→2 conversion lifts in [segment] by [date]"), written before the launch like any RFC mechanism and scored when the funnel read lands. **Message pull-through** is the qualitative check beside it: the positioning language appearing in customers' own mouths in win/loss interviews.

This is what keeps the discipline honest. The failure mode it prevents is being judged on whether sales likes the deck rather than whether the message wins deals, which builds fluency and zero judgement. Full PMM craft profile, exposures, and measures: [Craft](./craft.md).
